Local citations have been part of local SEO since the beginning. In 2026, they remain relevant but their role has evolved. This guide covers what you actually need to know: which citations matter, how to build them efficiently, and how to avoid wasting time on tactics that no longer work.

## What Are Local Citations?

A local citation is any online mention of your business that includes your:

- **N**ame
- **A**ddress
- **P**hone number

These are called NAP citations. They appear on business directories, social platforms, data aggregators, industry sites, and local publications.

**Examples of citation sources:**
- Google Business Profile
- Yelp, Yellow Pages, BBB
- Facebook, LinkedIn, Apple Maps
- Industry directories (Avvo for lawyers, Healthgrades for doctors)
- Chamber of Commerce websites
- Local news and event sites

## Do Citations Still Matter in 2026?

Yes, but less than before. Here's the current landscape:

**What citations do:**
- Provide trust signals to search engines (consistent NAP = legitimate business)
- Drive direct traffic and referrals from directory searches
- Support Google Business Profile verification

**What citations don't do (anymore):**
- Significantly boost rankings on their own
- Compensate for weak GBP optimization
- Replace the need for reviews and content

**Modern priority order:**
1. Google Business Profile optimization
2. Review quantity and quality
3. On-page local content
4. Backlinks from local/relevant sites
5. Citation consistency (we're here)

Citations are table stakes, not a ranking boost. Inconsistent citations hurt you. Consistent citations avoid penalties. Building hundreds more citations doesn't help much.

## Types of Citations

### Structured Citations

Listings on directories with standardized formats. Your business appears in a database with consistent fields.

**Examples:**
- Google Business Profile
- Yelp
- Yellow Pages
- Apple Maps
- Bing Places
- Facebook Business

**Importance:** High. These are the core citations every business needs.

### Unstructured Citations

Mentions of your business in articles, blog posts, news coverage, or other content where NAP appears but isn't in a standardized directory format.

**Examples:**
- Local newspaper article mentioning your business
- Blog post recommending local services
- Event page listing sponsors
- Local resource guides

**Importance:** Medium. Harder to build but provide contextual relevance.

### Data Aggregators

Companies that distribute business information to hundreds of directories. Getting listed with aggregators populates citations across the web.

**Major aggregators:**
- Data Axle (formerly Infogroup)
- Localeze (Neustar)
- Foursquare
- Factual

**Importance:** High for initial citation foundation. One submission reaches many directories.

## NAP Consistency: The Critical Factor

Your name, address, and phone number must be identical everywhere. Not similar. Identical.

### Common Inconsistency Problems

**Name variations:**
- "Joe's Plumbing" vs "Joe's Plumbing LLC" vs "Joe's Plumbing Services"
- Using DBA name on some sites, legal name on others

**Address variations:**
- "123 Main Street" vs "123 Main St" vs "123 Main St."
- Suite numbers missing or formatted differently
- Unit vs Suite vs # formats

**Phone variations:**
- Local number vs toll-free number
- Different tracking numbers on different sites
- Old numbers still listed

### How to Standardize NAP

1. **Choose one format and document it:**
   ```
   Business Name: Joe's Plumbing LLC
   Address: 123 Main Street, Suite 100
   City, State ZIP: Austin, TX 78701
   Phone: (512) 555-1234
   ```

2. **Match Google Business Profile exactly**
3. **Update everything else to match**
4. **Use the same format for new submissions**

## Managing Citations Over Time

## Common Citation Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Building Before Fixing

Building new citations with inconsistent NAP spreads the problem. Audit and fix first.

### Mistake 2: Using Cheap Mass-Submission Services

Services promising "500 citations for $99" often submit to spam directories that hurt more than help. Quality over quantity.

### Mistake 3: Ignoring Duplicate Listings

Multiple listings on the same platform confuse search engines. Find and merge duplicates.

### Mistake 4: Set and Forget

Citations drift over time as data syncs imperfectly. Schedule annual audits minimum.

### Mistake 5: Overemphasizing Citations

Citations are one factor among many. If you're spending more time on citations than GBP optimization or reviews, your priorities are wrong.

## FAQ

### How many citations do I need?

Quality beats quantity. 40-50 accurate citations on relevant directories is sufficient for most local businesses. Building hundreds on spam directories wastes time and can hurt.

### How long does citation building take to impact rankings?

Data aggregators take 2-6 months to propagate. Ranking impact (if any) typically appears 3-6 months after citations are consistent.

### Should I pay for citation building services?

For multi-location businesses or agencies, yes. Manual submission takes 30-60 minutes per directory. Services like BrightLocal's Citation Builder ($2/citation) or Whitespark's Listings Service save significant time.

### Are free citation sites worth submitting to?

Major free directories (Google, Apple, Bing, Facebook, Yelp) are essential. Lesser-known free directories vary — check domain authority and actual traffic before spending time.

### What if my old business address is everywhere?

This is common after moves. Prioritize:
1. Update Google Business Profile
2. Update data aggregators
3. Update major directories
4. Let aggregator updates propagate
5. Monitor and manually fix remaining issues

### Should I use a tracking phone number for citations?

Generally no. Use your primary business number for NAP consistency. If you need call tracking, use dynamic number insertion on your website instead of different numbers across directories.
